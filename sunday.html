Overview

Sunday isn't purely restful. It's a transitional emotional field where comfort, preparation, and anticipatory stress overlap.

Sunday Rituals is a web app designed to address that specific moment of vulnerability — the emotional shift from weekend rest into anticipatory Monday dread. The goal wasn't to eliminate the Sunday Scaries. It was to reframe Sunday as a structured emotional ritual: something to move through intentionally, not something to dread.

The app moves users through three emotional phases: calm and grounded through intention-setting, controlled mental activation through self-paced games, and acceptance of transition through language and tone that acknowledges the week ahead without catastrophising it.

Research

Three methods. One consistent finding: Sunday is emotionally layered in a way most apps completely ignore.

01

Cultural Probe

Participants photographed objects, rituals, and moments from their Sundays. The responses revealed three dominant emotional themes: rest and sensory comfort (comfy clothes, couch, sleep in), preparation and control (meal prep, planning, finishing work), and anticipatory transition (wind-down rituals coexisting with the awareness that tomorrow is Monday). Sunday is a sensory decompression zone where relaxation and obligation collide.

02

Emotional Journey Mapping

Mapping actions, touchpoints, and emotional states across Sunday's three phases revealed a consistent arc. Morning is calm and pleasant — low arousal, positive valence. Mid-day activates toward productivity while maintaining a sense of control. Evening tips into heightened, tense territory as the week becomes unavoidable. This progression directly shaped the app's flow: early screens are spacious and low-stimulation; later interactions introduce structure; language stays calming throughout.

03

Affect Grids

Using Russell's valence-arousal model, I plotted emotional states across the three time periods. Morning sat in the low-arousal, positive-valence quadrant. Mid-day shifted toward moderate arousal with positive valence. Evening moved toward higher arousal and neutral or negative valence — the Sunday Scaries in data form. This gave me quantitative grounding for every visual and interaction decision: reduce stimulation in early screens, increase gentle structure through mid-day, use language to counter the evening tension.

Synthesis

Across all three methods the same pattern emerged: Sunday begins restorative and ends tense. The emotional design challenge was to intervene in that arc — not by eliminating the tension, but by giving users a ritual to move through it.

Design Decisions

Every visual and interaction choice traces back to a specific emotional state on the affect grid.

Landing Page: Intention Setting

The entry point is a single question: "How do you want to feel today?" Nine word-choices — Peaceful, Energized, Grateful, Centered, Creative, Rested, Ready, Calm, Aligned — plus "Add your own" and "Skip." The design intentionally limits visible options to prevent overwhelm while preserving autonomy. Rounded components evoke softness. Large spacing reduces cognitive load. The subtitle "A moment of peace before the week begins" sets the emotional register for everything that follows.

Intention grounding shifts the user from Sunday stress into a moment of reflection. Minimal layout, large spacing, and rounded components all carry specific emotional intent.

Home Page: Games and the Sunday Boundary

Games only unlock on Sunday. This isn't a technical constraint — it's a design decision. By associating the rituals exclusively with Sunday, the app creates a contained emotional space. Users can leave it all behind until next week. The non-Sunday state is visually locked, which reduces anticipatory anxiety by making the boundary explicit rather than ambient.

Optional background "Sunday Sounds" audio can be toggled on — a calming audio layer that helps users ground themselves during the experience. A subtle "Your Sunday Journey" tracker shows games played and week streak using subdued highlight colours: controlled activation, not gamification excitement.

Solution

Three games, each designed to a different point on the emotional arc.

Sunday Self — Reflective Awareness

A short personality quiz rooted in Cultural Probe insights about identity and Sunday preparation. Three questions: "How are you feeling coming into Sunday?", "What is your ideal Sunday?", "By Sunday evening, you need to..." Results return a Sunday personality type with a summary of the user's journey. The result screen offers emotional reassurance — relationships fuel you, Sundays are for meaningful time with the people who matter most.

Sunday Pairs — Cognitive Grounding

A memory matching game using calming symbols (meditation pose, cloud, leaf). Deliberately no competitive scoring — moves are tracked, pairs are tracked, but there's no time pressure and no high score. The repetitive matching mechanic creates cognitive grounding through focused attention without escalating arousal. Clear visual hierarchy, minimal density, nothing that competes for attention.

Zen Garden — Countering Anticipatory Tension

A freeform creative space. Users drag elements across sand to create patterns, add objects, erase, and save or share their garden. Large negative space and intentionally limited interaction reduce stimulation. No objectives, no completion state. This game is designed for the evening — when the affect grid tips toward tension — as a way to re-centre before the week begins.

Sunday Rituals aims to address a moment of transition and anticipation and transform it into acceptance and growth.

— Critical Reflection, Spring 2026
Reflection

What I learned

→

Emotional states don't map cleanly onto UI components.

Translating valence-arousal theory into interface decisions required interpretive choices at every step. "Low arousal" doesn't mean "remove all interaction" — it means careful decisions about colour saturation, spacing, pacing, and visual density. Early wireframes were busier and unintentionally raised cognitive load. Iteration meant subtraction.

→

Structuring rest risks turning it into obligation.

The biggest tension in this project was the risk of making Sunday feel like another thing to optimise. Every feature had to ask: does this feel like a ritual, or does it feel like a task? The Sunday-only lock, the optional audio, the absence of competitive scoring — all of these were answers to that question.

→

The Cultural Probe surfaces what interviews miss.

Asking people to photograph their Sunday objects and rituals gave me data that no survey could have produced — the emotional texture of a couch, the specific anxiety of a planner left open. That material shaped every visual choice in the app. Qualitative methods that prioritise experience over efficiency produce more honest design constraints.
