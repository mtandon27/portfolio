Designing digital wellbeing through awareness, not control

Context & Background

We have the tools. So why are we still scrolling?

Despite years of screen-time features built into every smartphone like Apple's Screen Time and Android's Digital Wellbeing people continue to struggle with habitual phone use. The tools exist. The awareness is there. And yet the behavior persists.

This disconnect became our starting point. If the functionality already exists and people are still overusing their phones, the problem isn't technological. It's emotional.

The limits of "just set a timer"

Existing tools treat overuse as a rational failure: exceed a number, get a warning. But phone use is rarely rational. It's habitual, automatic, and emotionally driven — often triggered by stress, boredom, or fatigue rather than intent.

Two hours on Instagram late at night is not the same as two hours of focused reading during the day. Yet current systems treat them identically. The result? Interventions that arrive after the behavior has already taken hold, users who dismiss alerts without a second thought, and a persistent cycle of guilt that rarely leads to lasting change.

Pain 01

Interventions that interrupt, not integrate

Pop-ups arrive after a threshold is crossed — outside the behavior they're trying to change. By then, users are already in the flow.

Pain 02

One-size "treatments" for complex behavior

Time limits apply the same response to every situation, ignoring context, emotional state, and individual patterns.

Pain 03

Guilt without growth

Exceeding a limit triggers shame — not reflection. Users exit feeling bad but don't develop new habits or understanding.

Pain 04

Alerts get ignored over time

Repeated notifications create habituation. Users start dismissing them automatically — the very behavior the tool was meant to interrupt.

Design goal

We set out to build Soft Stop — a speculative mobile app that shifts the design focus from control to awareness. Rather than blocking behavior, Soft Stop responds to the texture of attention: how someone is engaging, not just for how long. Our goal was to surface moments of recognition within ongoing interaction, creating space for choice without imposing restriction.

Theoretical Framework

Grounding the work: emotion, habit, and the "machine zone"

Soft Stop is grounded in two bodies of theory that shaped every design decision we made.

Don Norman's Three Levels of Emotional Design

Norman (2004) describes how emotional responses to design operate at three interconnected levels. This framework organized our entire design language:

Visceral

First impressions and sensory response. We designed gentle, warm visual cues — gradual screen dimming, a soft companion emoji — that signal without alarming.

Behavioral

The feel of use. Reflective prompts, visual indicators, and intent-checking nudges help users understand their patterns in real time.

Reflective

Long-term meaning and memory. Weekly insights, mindful pause suggestions, and post-session summaries build a sense of progress over time.

Natasha Dow Schüll & The Machine Zone

Schüll's research on addictive technology describes a state of absorbed, dissociative interaction — the "machine zone" — where users lose awareness of time and intention. Behavior is no longer governed by choice, but by self-reinforcing loops. This framing helped us understand what we were actually designing against: not bad decisions, but the absence of decision-making entirely.

Rather than measuring time, Soft Stop tries to detect the behavioral signatures of that dissociative state — and gently surface users back out of it:

Signal 01

Unlock frequency — repeated checking behavior

Signal 02

App-switching rate — fragmented attention

Signal 03

Time-of-day patterns — contextual vulnerability

Signal 04

Scroll speed — intensity of engagement

Signal 05

Notification response time — immediacy of reaction

Signal 06

Repeated app re-opening — compulsive return

Individually, these signals are limited. Together, they begin to approximate the machine zone — and give Soft Stop a basis for intervening at the right moment, not just the right time.

Approach

Two methods, one goal: understand the emotional experience

We used two core emotional design methods, each building on the last, to move from identifying pain points to understanding the deeper emotional needs driving them.

01

Emotional Journey Mapping

We mapped the end-to-end emotional experience of using Apple's iOS Screen Time — from setup through the repeated pop-ups users encounter when a limit is hit. The mapping revealed three clear emotional low points: the first pop-up (frustration; users pick "15 more minutes" to resume quickly), the second pop-up (greater annoyance; users choose "Ignore for today"), and the final realization of having exceeded the limit (disappointment and self-doubt). What stood out wasn't just that emotions dropped — it was why they dropped. Each intervention felt punitive, not supportive. Users weren't learning anything; they were just feeling bad.

02

Inclusive / Affective Persona Analysis

We built and analyzed an affective persona — Lily L. — based on initial user interviews. Lily represents the younger generation's habitual phone use: opening Instagram when stressed, before bed, first thing in the morning. She knows her phone use is a problem. She already feels ashamed about it. Through Lily, we could see how much the emotional stakes mattered: she didn't need more restrictions — she needed something that felt like it was on her side. This persona grounded our design direction toward support over surveillance.

03

Initial User Testing (Round 1, ~4 participants)

We tested our first prototype iteration, asking participants to reflect on how visuals made them feel, whether the flow felt intuitive, and whether they trusted it. On the visceral level, all four participants responded positively to the calming color palette and layout. Behaviorally, they liked the onboarding but had questions about pop-up frequency. At the reflective level, they would recommend it to others — but felt it needed more personalized onboarding to fit individual goals. Critically: they did not want to feel controlled, even by a tool designed to help them.

What we learned

Insight 01

Feedback needs to feel supportive, not corrective

Users shut down when interventions feel like judgment. Reframing the tone from "you're doing this wrong" to "here's what you might not have noticed" changed everything.

Insight 02

Intent-checking creates space for reflection

A 20–30 second pause after opening an app — asking why you opened it — raises awareness before behavior becomes automatic.

Insight 03

Autonomy is non-negotiable

Users want help developing healthier habits on their own terms. Any system that feels like it's "in charge" triggers resistance, not cooperation.

Insight 04

Integration beats standalone

Users were more comfortable with Soft Stop embedded within iOS Settings than as a separate app. Familiarity reduced friction and increased trust.

Users want reminders that feel supportive and encouraging — not repetitive interruptions that are easy to dismiss.

Design Iterations

Testing two directions, three prototypes, and one key pivot

With our research insights in hand, we developed two onboarding concepts and three functional prototypes to test how Soft Stop could live within users' existing routines.

Onboarding: Standalone vs. Embedded

We tested whether Soft Stop should introduce itself as a new standalone app or live quietly within iOS Screen Time settings. Both had distinct tradeoffs.

Flow 01 — Standalone App

More onboarding control, higher friction

Users could build a personalized setup from scratch — choosing apps, vulnerability windows, time limits, and visual preferences. Rich, but it asked for a bigger commitment upfront.

Flow 02 — Inside iOS Settings

Familiar, lower-stakes, immediately trusted

Embedding Soft Stop within the existing Screen Time panel felt like a natural extension. Users didn't have to decide to "try a new app" — it was already part of a system they knew.

Outcome: Users responded significantly better to the integrated flow. The familiarity reduced resistance, and the lighter lift made it feel like a safe experiment rather than a full commitment. We moved forward with Flow 02.

Functional Prototypes: How Should Intervention Feel?

We built three prototypes that showed Soft Stop in action during active use — specifically, while scrolling Instagram. Each explored a different theory of how to intervene without being intrusive.

P1

Friction + Stats

A mid-scroll interruption with usage data (scrolled 410 ft, 30 min in feeds). Explicit and data-rich, but users found it too disruptive — the stats felt punitive rather than informative.

P2

Intent-First Onboarding + Limit Feedback

Longer onboarding that asked about goals, feelings, and app habits before setting limits. More personalized, but users wanted the nudges to be lighter during active use.

P3

Companion Creature + Gradual Visual Fading

A floating emoji companion in the Dynamic Island that changes expression as screen time accumulates. Paired with progressive screen desaturation. Users responded most positively — "it noticed without yelling at me."

Pivotal Learning

The most effective interventions made overuse visible without assigning blame. Emotional tone, not information density, determined whether users engaged or dismissed.

Final Design

A gentle companion, not a gatekeeper

The final prototype brings together everything we learned into a system that feels less like a restriction and more like a quiet, attentive friend who notices when you've drifted.

Key Design Decisions

①

Emoji companion in the Dynamic Island

As screen time accumulates, a small emoji appears in the Dynamic Island — neutral at first, then gently shifting expression. It doesn't pop up demanding attention. It's just there, quietly reflecting the user's state back at them. Users described it as "it noticed without yelling at me." This addresses the core failure of existing tools: awareness surfaces within the experience, not after it's over.

②

Progressive visual fading

As users approach their self-set limit, the interface gently desaturates — not a hard stop, but a gradual shift in the environment itself. This visceral-level design makes the invisible state of extended use literally visible, without any text or interruption.

③

Intent-check at app open

When users open a tracked app, Soft Stop surfaces a brief pause: "Before you scroll — you said you wanted to sleep better." This 20–30 second moment of friction is intentional. It interrupts the automatic motion of opening an app and creates space for a choice. Not a block — a breath.

④

Mindful pause, not hard stop

When the soft limit is reached, instead of a wall, users see: "Time for a soft stop." They're offered three micro-actions — Hydrate, Meditate, Self-care — and a 5-minute break timer. The experience is actionable and forward-looking. It ends with a box-breathing exercise and a reflection summary. This is the heart of the behavioral and reflective design levels working together.

⑤

Personalized, iOS-integrated onboarding

Setup lives inside iOS Screen Time settings — familiar territory. Users answer questions about how they want to feel, what they're scrolling away from, and which apps concern them most. The result is a system that feels built for them, not deployed at them.

Reflection

What this project taught me about designing for human behavior

Emotional tone is a design material.

We spent considerable time refining not what information to show users, but how it should feel when it arrived. The difference between a pop-up that shames and one that supports is almost entirely tonal — same message, completely different emotional response. I'll bring that sensitivity to every interaction I design going forward.

Designing for agency requires restraint.

The instinct in wellbeing design is to intervene — to add friction, force reflection, block access. But our users taught us that the moment a system feels controlling, it loses the user entirely. The most impactful design choices we made were things we decided not to do: not block, not shame, not require. Restraint turned out to be our most powerful tool.

Behavior change lives at the visceral level, not the reflective one.

We initially focused heavily on reflective features: summaries, insights, habit tracking. But users responded most to things they felt before they could name — the gradual dimming, the quiet companion, the pause before scrolling. This project shifted my design philosophy: if you want to change behavior, reach people at the level where behavior actually happens.

A good design asks the question, not just answers it.

The intent-check moment — that 20-second pause asking why you opened this app — became the soul of Soft Stop. It doesn't tell users what to do. It just creates the conditions for them to notice what they're doing. That distinction, between a tool that controls and a tool that creates space, is something I'll carry into every future project.

Digital wellbeing isn't a productivity problem. It's an emotional design challenge.
